### Persona 4: Michael Rodriguez - Elementary School Principal

**Demographics:**
- **Age:** 48
- **Location:** Phoenix, AZ (Maryvale neighborhood)
- **Role:** Principal at Sunnydale Elementary (K-5, 450 students)
- **Education:** Master's in Educational Leadership from Arizona State University, Bachelor's in Elementary Education
- **Experience:** 20 years in education (12 years teaching, 8 years as principal)
- **Salary:** $78,000/year
- **Family:** Married to Carmen (nurse), three children (ages 14, 16, 19)
- **Tech Savviness:** Medium (6/10)

**Professional Background:**
Michael Rodriguez grew up in Maryvale and returned to serve his community as an educator. He taught 5th grade for 12 years before becoming assistant principal, then principal. Sunnydale Elementary serves a predominantly Hispanic, working-class community where 85% of students qualify for free/reduced lunch.

Michael is deeply committed to providing his students with opportunities they might not otherwise have. Field trips are a crucial part of that mission - many of his students have never left their neighborhood, let alone visited a museum or science center. But with a tight budget ($15,000/year for all field trips across 18 classrooms), every dollar must count.

He's a caring, hands-on principal who knows every student by name. He's in classrooms daily, eats lunch with students, and attends their soccer games. But he's also responsible for budgets, compliance, safety, and managing 25 teachers. Field trip approval is just one of dozens of responsibilities competing for his attention.

**Personal Life:**
Michael and Carmen live in a modest home in Maryvale, close to the school. Their oldest son is in college (first in the family), their daughter is a high school junior, and their youngest is in 8th grade. Money is tight with college expenses, but they're proud of their kids' achievements.

Michael works 60+ hour weeks during the school year. He's at school by 6:30 AM and rarely leaves before 5:00 PM. He brings work home most nights and weekends. Carmen worries about his stress levels and encourages him to delegate more, but Michael feels personally responsible for every student's success.

He's active in his church and the local community. He coaches youth soccer on weekends and serves on the neighborhood council. He's respected in the community as someone who genuinely cares and works hard.

**Goals & Motivations:**

**Primary Goals:**
1. **Maximize Educational Value** - Ensure every trip directly supports learning standards and curriculum
2. **Budget Management** - Stay within $15,000 annual budget while serving 18 classrooms fairly
3. **Student Safety** - Ensure all trips meet safety requirements and have proper supervision
4. **Equity** - Give all students equal access to field trips regardless of ability to pay
5. **Efficiency** - Reduce time spent on trip approval from 2+ hours/week to under 30 minutes

**Secondary Goals:**
- Track which teachers utilize field trips effectively
- Ensure trips are distributed fairly across grade levels
- Reduce liability risk
- Improve communication with parents about trips
- Document educational outcomes for district reporting

**Deep Motivations:**
Michael is motivated by the memory of his own 4th grade field trip to the Grand Canyon - the first time he'd left Phoenix. That trip expanded his worldview and inspired him to become a teacher. He wants every student at Sunnydale to have those horizon-expanding experiences. He's also motivated by a sense of duty to his community and a desire to prove that schools in working-class neighborhoods can provide excellent education.

**Pain Points & Frustrations:**

**Critical Pain Points:**
1. **Email Chaos** - Teachers submit trip requests via email with varying levels of detail, hard to track
2. **Budget Blindness** - No real-time view of spending, discovers budget problems too late
3. **Approval Bottleneck** - Teachers wait days for approval while Michael juggles other priorities
4. **Incomplete Information** - Teachers often forget to include costs, chaperone plans, or curriculum alignment
5. **Equity Concerns** - Some teachers take multiple trips while others take none
6. **Safety Anxiety** - Worries about liability if something goes wrong on a trip
7. **Documentation Burden** - District requires documentation of educational value, hard to collect

**Emotional Frustrations:**
- Guilty when he has to deny trip requests due to budget
- Stressed by competing priorities and lack of time
- Worried about making mistakes that could harm students
- Frustrated by lack of visibility into trip outcomes
- Anxious about budget running out before year ends
- Disappointed when he can't give teachers quick answers

**Specific Scenarios:**

**Scenario 1: The Budget Crisis**
In February, Michael discovered they'd spent $12,000 of their $15,000 budget with 4 months remaining. He had to tell 6 teachers they couldn't take planned trips. Teachers were upset, students were disappointed, and Michael felt terrible. The problem: he'd been approving trips one-by-one via email without tracking cumulative spending. He'd been meaning to update his budget spreadsheet weekly but kept getting pulled into other crises. Now he checks the spreadsheet obsessively and second-guesses every approval, which slows down the process even more.

**Scenario 2: The Liability Nightmare**
Last year, a student broke his arm on a field trip to a rock climbing gym. The parents threatened to sue, claiming inadequate supervision. Michael spent weeks dealing with lawyers, insurance, and district officials. He had to prove they'd followed all safety protocols, but his documentation was scattered across emails and paper forms. Since then, he's paranoid about trip safety and asks teachers endless questions before approving trips, which frustrates them. He wishes he had a system that ensured all safety requirements were met before he even saw the request.

**Scenario 3: The Equity Problem**
At a staff meeting, a teacher pointed out that Ms. Johnson's 3rd grade class had taken 4 trips while Mr. Lee's 5th grade class had taken none. Michael hadn't realized the disparity. He felt embarrassed and worried about fairness. He wants to ensure all students get equal opportunities, but he has no easy way to track trip distribution across classrooms. He started a spreadsheet to track this but hasn't kept it updated. He knows there's a better way but doesn't have time to figure it out.

- **Location:** Seattle, WA (Capitol Hill)
- **Role:** Curriculum Director for Seattle Public Schools District 3 (12 elementary schools, 8,000 students)
- **Education:** EdD in Curriculum & Instruction from University of Washington, Master's in Education Policy
- **Experience:** 30 years in education (15 years teaching, 10 years as curriculum specialist, 5 years as director)
- **Salary:** $135,000/year
- **Family:** Married to Thomas (retired teacher), two adult children (ages 28 and 31), one grandchild
- **Tech Savviness:** Medium-High (7/10)

**Professional Background:**
Dr. Jennifer Wu has dedicated her career to ensuring equitable access to quality education. She started as a 3rd grade teacher in Seattle's most diverse neighborhoods and witnessed firsthand how field trips could transform students' perspectives. After earning her doctorate, she moved into curriculum development and eventually became District Curriculum Director.

Jennifer oversees 12 elementary schools serving 8,000 students across Seattle's most economically and ethnically diverse district. She manages a district-wide field trip budget of $180,000 and must ensure it's distributed equitably. She's also responsible for compliance with state learning standards, safety regulations, and district policies. She reports directly to the Assistant Superintendent and presents quarterly to the school board.

Jennifer is known as a champion for equity and data-driven decision making. She's published research on experiential learning and speaks at education conferences. She's respected but also feared - principals know she'll hold them accountable for outcomes.

**Personal Life:**
Jennifer and Thomas live in a comfortable home in Capitol Hill. Their children are grown and successful (one is a teacher, one is a doctor), and they recently became grandparents.
